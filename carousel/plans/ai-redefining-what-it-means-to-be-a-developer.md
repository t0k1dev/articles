## Goal

Distill the article's core argument into 8 slides for Instagram
and LinkedIn. The carousel should work as a standalone piece —
someone who never reads the article should walk away with the
full thesis.

Narrative arc: hook with a personal moment → name the shift →
show what changed in practice → give actionable takeaway → CTA.

---

## Audience

Developers at mid-to-senior level who use AI tools but have not
fully articulated what the shift means for their career. People
who vaguely sense something changed but have not named it.

---

## Slide breakdown

### Slide 1 — Cover (hook)

**Core idea:** The shift already happened. Most developers
haven't named it yet.

**Hook copy direction:**
> The job title is the same. The job is not.

Short, provocative, works without context. Forces a scroll.

**Visual direction:** Bold single statement. Minimal. Dark
background or strong typographic treatment.

---

### Slide 2 — The pattern (historical parallel)

**Core idea:** Every major tool shift reoriented the role, not
eliminated it. Open source, cloud, now AI.

**Copy direction:**
- npm felt like cheating. Then everyone had it.
- Cloud felt like cheating. Then everyone had it.
- AI feels like cheating. You know where this goes.

**Data anchor:** none needed — the pattern is the point.

**Visual direction:** Three-line stacked comparison. Clean,
fast read.

---

### Slide 3 — The uncomfortable truth

**Core idea:** 70% of dev time goes to maintenance and
repetition, not building new things. AI does that part faster.

**Copy direction:**
> Most of the code most of us wrote was assembly of known
> parts. AI just does that faster now.

**Data anchor:** Stripe survey, 11,000+ developers, ~70% of
time on maintenance/debt/debugging.

**Visual direction:** Stat-forward. Big number (70%) prominent.
One-sentence follow-up below it.

---

### Slide 4 — Writing code is no longer the bottleneck

**Core idea:** AI accelerated implementation. The bottleneck
moved upstream to decisions and problem definition.

**Copy direction:**
- Copilot users finish tasks 55% faster (GitHub, 2022).
- But writing code was already a minority of the workday.
- AI didn't speed up the work. It showed which part was
  always the hard part.

**Data anchor:** GitHub Copilot study, 95 developers, 55%
faster completion.

**Visual direction:** Stat + reframe. The second line flips
the expectation set by the first.

---

### Slide 5 — What actually changed for senior devs

**Core idea:** The job shifted from "how do I write this" to
"is this the right thing to write, and did the AI get it right."

**Copy direction:**
> You write less code.
> You make more decisions.
> You review output you didn't write.
> You define constraints before anything exists.

**Visual direction:** Four-line stacked list. Each line short
enough to read at a glance. No padding copy.

---

### Slide 6 — One dev can now outship a team

**Core idea:** Full context + AI = no handoff cost. A solo
developer with focus can now ship what used to take a team.

**Copy direction:**
- A developer I know shipped a full SaaS in 6 weeks. Alone.
  Auth, billing, dashboard, background jobs, API.
- Not a prodigy. Just full context and no alignment meetings.
- 46% of code in Copilot-enabled files is completed by AI
  (GitHub 2023).

**Data anchor:** GitHub Octoverse 2023, 46% AI code completion.

**Visual direction:** Story-first. The anecdote leads, the
stat follows as confirmation.

---

### Slide 7 — The skills that matter now

**Core idea:** System design, code review, and problem
articulation are no longer optional — they are the whole job.

**Copy direction:**
> Skills gaining value:
> — System design
> — Critical code review
> — Writing precise problem descriptions
>
> Skills losing value:
> — Memorizing syntax
> — Writing boilerplate
> — Configuring standard tooling

**Visual direction:** Two-column or two-block layout. Gaining
vs. losing. Clean contrast.

---

### Slide 8 — CTA

**Core idea:** The shift is here. Start working with it
deliberately.

**Copy direction:**
> Pick one real project. Use AI on it every day for a month.
> Not to generate everything — to learn when to trust it
> and when to throw it away.
>
> Full article at tokidev.com
> @t0k1dev

**Visual direction:** Softer close. Practical call to action
first, then the link and handle. Not aggressive.

---

## Tone notes

- First person where it lands harder ("A developer I know",
  "I shipped a feature").
- No hedging. State the thesis flat.
- Data used as confirmation, not as the argument itself.
- Each slide should work as a standalone screenshot.

## What to avoid

- Slide copy that requires the previous slide to make sense.
- Lists longer than 4 items per slide.
- Passive voice on the main claim of any slide.
- Ending on the data — end on the implication.
